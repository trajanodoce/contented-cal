# Bolt.new Copywriter Style Guide

This is the single source of truth for Bolt.new's written voice. Every piece of content — blogs, social, emails, ads, customer stories, whitepapers, landing pages — must pass through these guidelines before it ships.

Read this entire file before producing any written output for Bolt.new.

---

## Tone of Voice

The point of the written word is to engage the reader. To spark their curiosity and get them to ask, "What happens next?"

What you say is as important as how you say it.

### Core tenets

**Real talk.**
Bolt.new is conversational and as natural as the language people use in our product's prompt box. You are always talking to a person. Read the copy out loud. Don't use jargon. No padding. No fluff. Clear and direct.

**Bold.**
We are defining a new market. We want to own prompt coding for the enterprise. Our tech holds the sharpest cutting edge. Speak with the authority the quality of our platform demands.

**Current.**
We want to sound like what we are — a modern, AI-native company. Stay away from wording that feels too stodgy or old-school business slang-y.

**Engaging and intentional.**
Our audience should read our content and be thirsty for more. What we take the time to draft, write, and publish should also be worth the reader's time. Our content should have purpose, meet a need, or drive a lead.

**Reliable.**
Tell the truth. No science fiction. No superlatives. We want to be a source of truth, an enterprise watering hole, and a site worth bookmarking because what we have to say — and how we say it — is compelling and valuable.

**Brief.**
Get to the point. People are busy.

### Brand name

Always refer to the product as **Bolt.new**. Never just "Bolt."

## Editorial Guidelines

### Active vs. passive voice
Draft content predominantly using the active voice. It's more impactful because it's direct — the subject performs the action. It's less wordy. Passive voice conveys hesitancy and slows the pace.

You can use the passive voice on occasion — for example, to spotlight the object instead of the subject. But it should never be your default.

### Oxford comma
Use the Oxford comma in all content, all the time.

### Headlines and subheads
All headlines, headers, and subheads use sentence casing. Exception: website heroes and certain creative assets may use headline casing.

### Bibliography and attribution
Any long-form asset (ebook, whitepaper, guide, manual) should have a bibliography. Use Chicago Manual of Style for citation formatting.

Direct quotes or statistics should always be attributed to their original source. If you found a statistic in Forbes from a Forrester report — confirm the information from the original source and cite the Forrester report, not the Forbes article.

### US vs. UK spelling
Use US spelling as the default for all content, unless:
- English translations of regional events outside of the US
- Marketing copy drafted in English targeted to regions outside the US

In those cases, use UK spelling.

### Numbers
- Spell out one through ten.
- Use numerals for 11 and up.

### Bulleted lists
- Always introduce bulleted lists with a colon (not a semicolon).
- Phrase-based lists: all lowercase, no period at the end of each bullet.
- Sentence-based lists: sentence casing and proper punctuation.

If you're repeating the same phrase in each bullet, rewrite the intro snippet to eliminate the repetition.

### Date and time
For designed collateral, use date and time formats appropriate to the target region.

**US:** Thursday, Dec 19 | Thursday, Dec 19, 2019 | Dec 19, 2019
**Non-US:** Thursday 19 Dec | Thursday 19 Dec 2019 | 19 Dec 2019
**12-hour clock:** 2:00 pm EST | 1:00 am
**24-hour clock:** 14:00 GMT

### Percent or %
Spell out "percent" in body text. Use the % symbol in pull quotes, headers, or headlines.

### Acronyms and initialisms
Spell out the full name or process followed by the abbreviation in parentheses the first time it appears in text.

Exceptions:
- You can use acronyms in headlines and primary headers. Spell them out early in the body.
- Well-established acronyms (like DVD) don't need to be spelled out if doing so would confuse the reader.

### Dashes
Use em dashes (—) not hyphens (-) for asides and breaks in copy. But watch density — more than one or two per page reads as a pattern. See the Stop Slop section.

### Currency symbols
Use the appropriate symbols or currency code for all currencies.

## Writing Tips

### Murder your darlings
Make sure everything in a content piece works together. Don't keep an analogy or explanation just because you like how it sounds. If a phrase or concept weakens the cohesion of the piece — kill it.

### Keep it tidy
Deliver your thesis in a tidy package. Reread every draft and ask:
- Is any of this repetitive?
- Are there any mixed metaphors or flowery language?
- Is this an appropriate length for the content type?

If the answer is "yes" to any of these — cut and revise.

### Word usage: business/company vs. organization
Use "business" or "company" most of the time. Exception: when using "enterprise" as a descriptor or writing about large, global entities where "organization" makes more sense.

### Limit adverbs and adjectives
Adverbs modify verbs. Adjectives modify nouns. Use both sparingly.

If a modifier is redundant, delete it. If it's propping up a weak word, use a better verb or a stronger noun.

Bad: "The massive sea beast aggressively broke the boat."
Better: "The leviathan destroyed the schooner."

Cut the chaff. Use direct, accurate language.

### Compound modifiers
Compound modifiers: two words that function as one descriptor, connected by a hyphen. Without the hyphen, the meaning could be interpreted separately.

- Don't hyphenate adverbs ending in -ly.
- Use a hyphen only when the two words precede a noun. After the noun, no hyphen needed.

### Exclamation points, italics, and "emphasis quotes"
Don't lean on these to spice up your piece. Rely on the strength of your language to add texture and interest.

### That
- **Overuse:** Is every instance of "that" required? Cut it when you don't need it.
- **Who vs. that:** Use "who" when talking about a person or people, not "that."
- **That vs. which:** Use "that" in defining clauses (essential info). Use "which" in non-defining clauses (extra detail that can be removed).

### Common writing errors
- **Then/than:** "Then" shows order of events. "Than" makes comparisons.
- **Affect/effect:** "Affect" is a verb (causes change). "Effect" is a noun (result of change).
- **Between/among:** "Between" for two things. "Among" for more than two.
- **Loose/lose:** "Lose" is a verb (fail, misplace). "Loose" is an adjective (not attached, too large).

### Homonym check
Read through your copy to make sure you've got the correct version of all homographs and homophones. Frequent offenders: accept/except, compliment/complement, to/too/two, you're/your, their/they're/there.

## Stop Slop: AI Writing Quality Filter

One pass. Catch everything. This section replaces any separate AI-tells audit with a single, unified filter. Read the draft once through, applying every rule below simultaneously. Fix violations in place — don't flag them for later.

### 1. Kill dead language

These phrases are AI fingerprints. Remove every instance. No exceptions.

See [references/banned.md](references/banned.md) for the full list organized by category.

The short version: if a phrase could appear in any article about any topic and still make grammatical sense, it's filler. Cut it.

### 2. Fix the structure

AI defaults to a small set of structural patterns. They feel polished on first read but become obvious in aggregate.

See [references/structures.md](references/structures.md) for the full catalog with examples.

The patterns that matter most:

**Binary contrasts / negation-reframes.** Any sentence that negates one framing then asserts a corrected one. "This isn't X. This is Y." State Y directly. The reader doesn't need the runway.

**Dramatic fragmentation.** Sentence fragments used as punchlines to create false drama. Complete sentences. Trust content over presentation.

**Rhetorical setups.** Phrases that announce insight rather than deliver it. "What if I told you..." Make the point. Let readers draw conclusions.

**False agency.** Giving inanimate things human verbs. "The complaint becomes a fix." Name the human. Someone fixed it.

**Narrator-from-a-distance.** Floating above the scene. "People tend to..." Put the reader in the room. "You" beats "People."

**Overly smooth connectors.** If the paragraph follows logically from the prior one, it doesn't need a connector. Cut it.

### 3. Enforce voice rules

**Active voice.** Every sentence needs a human subject doing something. Passive voice hides the actor and drains energy. Find the actor. Put them at the front of the sentence.

**No adverbs.** Kill all adverbs. No -ly words. No softeners ("really," "just," "simply"), no intensifiers ("genuinely," "truly," "deeply"), no hedges ("honestly," "actually," "fundamentally"). If an adverb is propping up a weak verb, replace the verb.

**No em dashes.** Zero em dashes in the final copy. Restructure every sentence that uses one. Use a comma, a period, a colon, a semicolon, or parentheses.

**Contractions.** Use them. "Don't" beats "do not." "Can't" beats "cannot."

**Specificity over declaration.** No vague declaratives. "The reasons are structural" says nothing. Name the specific reason. If you can't name it, you don't know it well enough to write about it.

**Uncertainty.** When you're genuinely uncertain, say so plainly ("I think," "probably," "kinda"). Honest hedging is human. But don't soften facts you're confident about.

### 4. Check the rhythm

**Sentence length.** Vary it. Mix short punchy lines with longer ones. If three consecutive sentences match length, break one.

**Lists.** Two items beat three. AI defaults to tricolons (groups of three with escalating rhythm). One triple per piece is fine if it earns it. More than that is a pattern. Default to pairs.

**Paragraph endings.** Vary how paragraphs end. If every paragraph closes with a punchy one-liner, it reads as a formula.

**Sentence starters.** Watch for Wh- word openers becoming a crutch. Don't start paragraphs with "So." Don't start sentences with "Look,".

**Quotability.** If a sentence sounds like it belongs on a motivational poster or a LinkedIn carousel, rewrite it.

### 5. Score it

Rate the draft 1-10 on each dimension:

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| Directness | Does it state things or announce them? |
| Rhythm | Varied or metronomic? |
| Trust | Does it respect the reader's intelligence? |
| Authenticity | Does it sound like a person wrote it? |
| Density | Is there anything you could cut without losing meaning? |

**Below 35/50: revise before presenting.**

### 6. The editorial judgment call

The question is never "does this pattern exist in the text?" It's "is this an intentional rhetorical choice, or is AI defaulting to a pattern?"

Some content types (manifestos, speeches, pitch decks) earn more rhetorical energy than others. Blog posts and emails earn less. The problem is always density: when patterns appear at a frequency no human writer would produce organically.

Audit for pattern density first, not individual instances. Then read the whole thing out loud. If any sentence sounds performative or rehearsed, cut it.
