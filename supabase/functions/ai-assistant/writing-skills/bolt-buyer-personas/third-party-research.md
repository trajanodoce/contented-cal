## Persona 2a: CTO / App Dev Leader

### Key data points

- 84% of developers are using or planning to use AI tools, up from 76% the prior year. 47.1% use AI tools daily. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- Developer trust in AI is declining: 45.7% distrust AI accuracy. Only 3.1% "highly trust" AI output. Experienced developers show the lowest trust (2.6%). *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- In a randomized controlled trial, experienced developers were 19% slower when using AI tools vs. working without them — despite expecting a 24% speedup. *(METR 2025)*
- Even after experiencing the slowdown, developers still believed AI had sped them up by 20%. *(METR 2025)*
- Two-thirds of software firms have deployed GenAI tools, but developer adoption among those with access remains low. *(Bain 2025)*
- Three out of four companies say the hardest part is getting people to change how they work. *(Bain 2025)*
- Teams using AI assistants see 10-15% productivity boosts. Leading companies that pair AI with end-to-end process transformation report 25-30% gains. *(Bain 2025)*
- Code writing and testing account for only 25-35% of time from concept to product launch. *(Bain 2025)*
- 62% of organizations are at least experimenting with AI agents; 23% are scaling agentic AI somewhere in the enterprise, 39% experimenting. Scaling is usually limited to one or two functions. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- 51% of organizations experienced at least one negative consequence from AI; inaccuracy is the most common. Organizations now mitigate an average of four AI-related risks, up from two in 2022. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- 64% say AI enables innovation, but only 39% report any EBIT impact at the enterprise level — and most of that is under 5% of EBIT. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- Only ~6% of organizations qualify as AI "high performers" (attributing 5%+ EBIT impact). These orgs are more likely to redesign workflows, scale agents, have senior leadership ownership, and invest 20%+ of digital budgets in AI. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- Cost benefits most commonly reported in software engineering, manufacturing, and IT; revenue benefits most often in marketing/sales, strategy/corporate finance, and product/service development. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- 32% expect AI to reduce their organization's workforce by at least 3% in the next year; 43% expect little or no change. Larger organizations are more likely to expect reductions. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- Self-admitted GenAI usage in open-source projects showed no widespread code quality degradation — many projects showed improvement in code churn metrics. *(arXiv 2025)*
- 6 of 151 analyzed repositories had prohibitive GenAI policies (complete bans); 4 had restrictive policies (disclosure requirements). *(arXiv 2025)*

1. **"Developers expected a 24% speedup from AI tools but experienced a 19% slowdown — and still believed AI had helped."** — METR AI Developer Productivity Study, Jul 2025
2. **"Only 3.1% of developers highly trust AI-generated output. Among the most experienced developers, that number drops to 2.6%."** — Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025
3. **"Three out of four companies say the hardest part of AI adoption is getting people to change how they work."** — Bain, From Pilots to Payoff 2025

## Persona 2b: CPO (Chief Product Officer)

### Key data points

- In 2022, only 15% of Fortune 1000 companies had CPO roles. That number is now nearly 60%. *(Korn Ferry 2025)*
- 70% of CPOs now hold profit and loss responsibility. *(Korn Ferry 2025)*
- Companies with strong CPOs outperform the market by 35%. *(Korn Ferry 2025)*
- By 2030, 30% of CEOs will be former CPOs. *(Korn Ferry 2025)*
- Early AI adopters reported a 35% increase in innovation and a 50% reduction in development time. *(Korn Ferry 2025, citing Industrial Marketing Management 2024)*
- AI is pushing organizations from traditional pyramids to agile, diamond-shaped structures, with fewer layers of middle management. *(Korn Ferry 2025)*
- 88% of organizations now use AI in at least one function, up from 78% the prior year. 72% report using GenAI, up from 33% in 2024. But adoption is still shallow — most remain in experimentation or pilot phases, and only about one-third report scaling AI across the enterprise. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- 64% say AI enables innovation, but only 39% report any EBIT impact at the enterprise level. Only ~6% qualify as AI "high performers." *(McKinsey 2025)*
- High-performing organizations are more likely to redesign workflows, scale agents, have senior leadership ownership of AI initiatives, define human-validation processes, and invest 20%+ of digital budgets in AI. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- Cost benefits most commonly reported in software engineering, manufacturing, and IT; revenue benefits most often in marketing/sales, strategy/corporate finance, and product/service development. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- 32% expect AI-driven workforce reductions of 3%+ in the next year; 43% expect little or no change; 13% expect headcount increases. Larger orgs more likely to expect reductions. *(McKinsey 2025)*
- Even modest AI productivity gains don't translate into positive returns without redirecting time savings to higher-value work. *(Bain 2025)*
- 81% of leaders expect AI agents to be moderately or extensively integrated into their company's AI strategy in the next 12-18 months. *(Microsoft Work Trend Index 2025)*

## Persona 4: Professional developer

### Key data points

- Developer sentiment toward AI is declining: 59.7% hold positive views, down from 70%+ in 2023-2024. The "very favorable" segment dropped to 22.9%. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- 45.7% of developers distrust AI accuracy. Only 3.1% highly trust it. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- The biggest frustration: "AI solutions that are almost right, but not quite" (66%). Debugging AI code is the second biggest complaint (45.2%). *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- 20% report reduced confidence in their own problem-solving after using AI tools. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- In METR's randomized trial, 16 experienced developers averaging 22,000+ star repos were 19% slower using AI tools (Cursor Pro with Claude). The study compensated participants $150/hr and assigned 246 real issues. *(METR 2025)*
- Developer resistance factors: distrust of AI tools, concern AI undermines their professional role, tendency to revert to established habits under pressure. *(Bain 2025)*
- In open-source projects, 13 mentions of bug fixes to AI-generated code were identified; one developer noted "do not trust GitHub Copilot" regarding axis orientation. *(arXiv 2025)*
- Generation tasks showed higher rework indicators than optimization or maintenance activities. *(arXiv 2025)*
- Despite GenAI adoption, file-based code churn decreased (from 0.17 to 0.06) with medium effect size — no evidence of widespread quality degradation. *(arXiv 2025)*
- 52% of developers don't use AI agents at all; only 14.1% use them daily. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- LLM adoption among developers: OpenAI GPT (81.4%), Claude Sonnet (42.8%), Gemini Flash (35.3%). Claude Sonnet is more popular among professionals (45%) than learners (30%). *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- Claude Sonnet has a 67.5% admiration rate among developers who use it. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*
- VS Code dominates at 75.9%. Python (57.9%) gained 7 points year-over-year. *(Stack Overflow 2025)*

## Cross-persona themes

### The trust paradox
AI adoption is near-universal (78-88% of organizations), but trust is low and declining. Developers distrust accuracy (45.7%), creators worry about training data consent (69%), and marketers hit personalization barriers (98%). The tools are everywhere; confidence in them is not.

### The productivity perception gap
METR's randomized trial is the sharpest example: developers were measurably slower with AI but believed they were faster. Self-reported productivity gains (69-84% across surveys) may significantly overstate actual impact. The implication for content: lead with specific, measurable outcomes rather than vague productivity claims.

### The "almost right" problem
66% of developers cite "AI solutions that are almost right, but not quite" as their top frustration. This maps directly to every persona's experience — the prototype that needs to be rebuilt, the landing page that's close but not brand-right, the code that looks correct but breaks in edge cases.

### AI as table stakes, not differentiator
When 84-86% of an audience already uses AI tools, the tools themselves are no longer the advantage. Korn Ferry's finding for CPOs applies broadly: the differentiator is taste, vision, and strategic judgment — not access to AI. Content should position Bolt.new as the tool that elevates human direction, not the tool that replaces it.

### The scaling gap
McKinsey: only one-third of organizations have scaled AI beyond pilots, and just 6% qualify as high performers. 64% say AI enables innovation, but only 39% see any EBIT impact. Bain: even modest gains don't translate to ROI without process change. The gap between "we use AI" and "AI drives business results" is where most organizations are stuck — and where Bolt.new's full-app generation (vs. code-completion) value proposition is strongest.
